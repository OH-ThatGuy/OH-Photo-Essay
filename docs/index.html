Photo Essay

By: Owen Helm

Completed in 2001, the Larry and Kathryn Smith Chapel has become a place of worship for many students at Penn State Behrend. It’s located right on campus next to the Burke building. For any students living on campus, the chapel is only ever a short walk away. The chapel itself features a carillon made up of 48 bells; this wonderful instrument was gifted to the university along with the bell tower itself by the late Floyd and Juanita Smith. Since its creation, the chapel has been the site of various weddings, events, and other religious services.

When the chapel was first constructed, it was done so as a place of multi-faith worship, the building itself taking heavy influence from Christian faith. Within Christianity, it is taught that humanity must act as stewards of the land. Nearly every religion teaches the value of protecting one’s environment. Whether it be Christianity, Buddhism, or Islam, they all have some sort of doctrine involving nature and its upkeep. The caretakers of the Larry and Kathryn Smith Chapel know this well, and it’s apparent just by looking at it.

When observing the chapel and its surrounding environment, it’s noticeable right away how well the area is maintained. It’s almost as if the chapel grew there along with the grass and trees. There’s vegetation all over, with various flowers and other flora growing by the building. It’s also not uncommon to see wildlife roam about the area. Birds perch on nearby trees, squirrels and other rodents run around searching for food, and insects can be seen crawling in the grass. Nature is everywhere you look, but it would be disingenuous to say that everything is perfect.

Despite the care taken to preserve the nature surrounding the chapel, it’s hard not to notice the signs of human presence. Ignoring the chapel itself, the most obvious thing you’ll notice is the Burke building. While Burke is a well-made and frankly nice-looking building, it does stick out like a sore thumb compared to the brick-and-mortar architecture of the chapel. The various walkways around the chapel make human traversal quite easy, but insects who rely on grass to hide from predators can struggle. Speaking of grass, it’s cut regularly. It looks quite nice, but the grass is often cut quite low. Last but not least are the lamps that line the sidewalks. Light pollution is a growing concern among environmentalists, and the design of Behrend’s lamps doesn’t do anything to remedy it. Not only that, but the lamps also interfere with the natural cycles of nocturnal insects.

In conclusion, the Larry and Kathryn Smith Chapel stands as a testament to the relationship between faith and nature. It embodies the principles of stewardship that are taught across various religions. However, the presence of modern human interference is noticeable and reminds us of challenges that come with balancing nature and humanity. I am hopeful that the future of the chapel is bright, and the future of the world surrounding it even brighter.
